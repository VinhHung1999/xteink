## The Vietnamese User — A Specific Portrait

She is 22. She lives in HCMC. She is a university student or just graduated. She earns 5-10 million VND a month — enough for rent, food, and one or two small luxuries.

She spends 5.9 hours a day on her phone. She knows this because her phone tells her, and the number makes her wince. TikTok. Zalo. Instagram. Facebook. The cycle repeats. She opens one app, closes it, opens another, closes it, opens the first one again. She doesn't enjoy it anymore. It's just... what she does.

She has a BookTok list. Ten books she saved from videos — books that looked beautiful, that the creator described with shining eyes, that she thought would make her feel something. She hasn't read any of them. They sit in her "Saved" folder like promises she keeps breaking.

She is not lazy. She is not undisciplined. She is a young woman drowning in the most addictive technology ever created, surrounded by 79.8 million other Vietnamese internet users doing the same thing, and she is beginning to realize that scrolling is not living.

One day, she sees a TikTok. A small device attached to a phone. An E-Ink screen. Someone reading at a café — the light is golden, the coffee is iced, the scene is beautiful in that specific BookTok way that makes her heart ache a little. The person is reading. Not performing. Reading.

The caption says: "1.5 triệu. I read more in two weeks than in two years."

She checks the price. 1.5 triệu. She made that much last week from her part-time job. She doesn't think about it very long. She orders it.

She doesn't know it yet, but she just bought a permission slip.

## Why Vietnam, Why Now

Five facts that make Vietnam in 2026 the perfect market for this device:

### 1. They Already Read — On Their Phones

51% of all digital readers on Waka (Vietnam's largest ebook platform) are 18-24 years old. They read an average of 13 hours and 15 minutes per week. They read on their phones — not tablets, not laptops, not eReaders. Phones.

The X4 doesn't ask them to change devices. It **attaches to the device they already can't put down**. It meets them where they are.

### 2. They Are Exhausted by Their Screens

59% of Vietnamese 18-29 year olds spend more than 3 hours a day on social media. The average is closer to 6 hours. And the awareness is growing — Vietnam.vn (the government portal) has published dedicated coverage on digital detox. The exhaustion is real. The desire for something different is real.

But — and this is critical — Vietnamese youth are not anti-technology. They love technology. They are early adopters, enthusiastic, curious. They don't want a dumbphone. They don't want to go offline. They want a **smarter way to be online**. The X4 — a device that attaches to your phone, that adds a quiet layer to the noisy device — is exactly that.

### 3. BookTok Is Massive in Vietnam

2.5 billion views. 840,000 videos uploaded. The Vietnam Publishers Association signed a cooperation agreement with TikTok Vietnam. Books are content. Reading is aesthetic. "Currently reading" is identity.

The X4 is the most BookTok-friendly device ever created. It is photogenic. It has a story. It is obscure enough to make the poster look interesting, and cheap enough that followers can actually buy one. It is not an ad. It is lifestyle content that happens to be a product.

### 4. The Price Is Right

1.5 million VND. A student can afford it. A fresh graduate can afford it. A parent can buy it as a gift without stress. Compare: Kindle Basic at 2.8 triệu. Kobo Clara at 3.5 triệu. Boox Palma at 7.8 triệu.

At 1.5 triệu, the X4 is in impulse-buy territory for urban Vietnamese youth. The question is not "can I afford this?" but "why not?"

### 5. Nobody Else Is Here Yet

MayDocSachTot.com sells the X4 — 34 units. Zero reviews. No Vietnamese guide. No community. No content. No pre-configured device. The market essentially doesn't know this product exists.

There is no Vietnamese review of the X4. No Vietnamese YouTube tutorial. No Vietnamese community. The first person to create these things will own the market — not because they are the biggest, but because they are the **first**.

## The Vietnamese Emotional Landscape

The universal emotions from the previous files take on specific Vietnamese textures:

### Quiet — *Sự yên lặng*

In Vietnam, quiet is both rare and sacred. The cities are loud — motorbikes, construction, karaoke, street vendors. The digital world is louder — Zalo groups with 200 messages, Facebook feeds that never end, TikTok videos auto-playing one after another.

The X4's quiet is not just a feature. In Vietnam, it is **a luxury that costs less than a cup of bạc xỉu**.

### Return — *Sự trở về*

Vietnamese youth grew up with Nokia phones before smartphones arrived. The click of a physical button is not just nostalgic — it is the sound of childhood. Before smartphones, before TikTok, before the exhaustion. The X4's buttons carry this memory in Vietnamese hands differently than in American ones — because the distance between then and now feels shorter, and the loss feels more personal.

### Ownership — *Sở hữu*

In a country where pirated content was historically the norm and licensed digital content is still maturing, the X4's DRM-free philosophy resonates differently. Vietnamese users already sideload. They already know how to find EPUB files. They already have the skills. The X4 doesn't ask them to learn something new — it gives them a **legitimate, beautiful container for what they already do**.

dtv-ebook.com. isach.info. biblio.vn. nhasachmienphi.com. These are the libraries the X4 was built for, even if Xteink doesn't know it.

### Becoming — *Trở thành*

Vietnamese Gen Z is arguably the most ambitious generation in Vietnamese history. They study English to access the global economy. They read self-improvement books (21% of digital reading on Waka). They follow international productivity and mindset content.

The X4's identity promise — *"I am becoming a reader"* — speaks directly to this ambition. In Vietnam, reading is not just a hobby. It is a **class signal**. It says: I am educated. I am curious. I am going somewhere. A young Vietnamese person carrying an X4 is saying something to themselves and to the world: *tôi đang đọc sách, tôi đang trưởng thành* — I am reading, I am growing.

### Belonging — *Thuộc về*

Vietnam's community culture runs deep — Zalo groups, Facebook groups, neighborhood connections, school alumni networks. The X4 community, when built in Vietnam, will not look like the Reddit community. It will look like a **Zalo group where people share books, share screenshots of their reading progress, share photos of their X4 at cafés, and help each other troubleshoot at midnight**.

This is not customer support. This is *hội đọc sách* — a reading club. And in Vietnamese culture, belonging to a *hội* is belonging to a family.

## The Vietnamese Moments of Magic

Some moments hit differently in Vietnam:

### The Commute Moment

In HCMC, the average motorbike commute is 30-60 minutes. But for those who take buses — and the bus system is growing — the X4 transforms dead time into reading time. On a crowded bus, you cannot hold a Kindle. You can barely hold your phone. But the X4? One hand. Pocket-sized. Three pages before your stop.

### The Café Moment

Vietnamese café culture is the most vibrant in Southeast Asia. Sitting in a café with an iced coffee is not a luxury — it is a daily ritual for millions. The X4 attached to a phone, sitting on a wooden table, next to a cà phê sữa đá — this is the most natural image in the world. It doesn't need to be staged. It is already the vibe.

### The Gift Moment

Vietnamese gift-giving culture prizes thoughtfulness over expense. A 1.5 triệu gift that says *"I know you love reading, and I want to help you read more"* is worth more than a 5 triệu gift that says nothing. The X4 is the rare product that is both affordable and deeply meaningful as a gift — perfect for birthdays, graduations, Tết.

### The English Learning Moment

Vietnam has millions of young people actively studying English. Reading English novels is one of the most effective (and most recommended) ways to improve. An X4 loaded with English classics from Standard Ebooks or Project Gutenberg is not just a reading device — it is a **portable English classroom** that costs less than two months of language tutoring.
